Chinese Traditions

Double Happiness
The double happiness symbol (pictured below) is a symbol used only in Chinese weddings. Pronounced shuāngxǐ (双喜), meaning "double happiness," this symbol is two of the Chinese character for happiness combined. Items and decorations in Chinese weddings often come in pairs to symbolize the joining of the happy couple. Look for this symbol around the wedding, such as on the back of the invitation envelopes! Colors
Chinese weddings are typically decked out with red. The color red is a lucky color in China, associated with happiness and good fortune, so it is often used in celebrations, New Years, and, of course, weddings! While our wedding color is primarily purple (Jennifer's favorite color), we have included some red accents throughout the wedding. White is avoided at weddings because the color white is associated with funerals and death. However, in modern Chinese weddings, the bride may wear a white gown for the ceremony followed by a red gown for the banquet.
Chinese Rituals
Leading up to the wedding, Chinese traditions may include a series of trials for the groom and a tea ceremony. Although pre-wedding rituals can be elaborate, Chinese wedding ceremonies are simple and brief, as the main event is the wedding banquet!

Jewish Traditions

Ketubah
The ketubah is the Jewish marriage contract, written in egalitarian Aramaic and English. The Jewish wedding ceremony begins when the couple signs the ketubah in a private ceremony just before walking down the aisle to the chuppah.
Chuppah
The chuppah is the Jewish wedding canopy, which symbolizes our new home. The four sides of the chuppah are open so that the family and friends surrounding it feel welcome. The top of the chuppah will be covered with the tallit, which is the shawl that Dan was wrapped in as a newborn.
Breaking of the Glass
This final ritual in the Jewish wedding ceremony has many meanings. One interpretation is that a glass, once broken, is irrevocably changed. Marriage is also a transforming experience for the bride and groom.
Yichud (Seclusion)
Immediately following the ceremony, we will spend a few minutes alone in a private room, appreciating the significance of the ceremony and enjoying our first moments together as a married couple.
